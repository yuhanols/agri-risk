\title{Weather Shocks, Supply Dynamics, and Price Persistence: \\ Evidence from U.S.\ Lettuce Markets}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This paper constructs a weekly dataset that integrates USDA shipment data, FOB (free on board) prices, and weather data to study weather-driven supply and price dynamics in U.S.\ lettuce markets from 2010 to 2026. Using an empirical strategy that combines market-level aggregation for prediction with district-level panel variation for identification, we document three results. First, simple linear models outperform tree-based machine learning methods. OLS achieves substantially lower prediction error than XGBoost and Random Forest (RMSE \$4.36 versus \$6.67 and \$6.81), reflecting strong price persistence and a data-generating process that is largely linear conditional on lagged prices. Second, weather effects on prices are present but attenuated in aggregate data. In district-level panels, frost events increase weekly prices by \$2.33 ($p < 0.001$), while these effects are diluted in market-level regressions due to price persistence and spatial aggregation. Third, these patterns are robust across lettuce varieties, including Romaine, and across alternative validation strategies. These results show that downstream market outcomes, shipment volumes and FOB prices, can be used to identify weather-driven supply responses even in the absence of direct yield measures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Fresh produce supply chains are exposed to weather risk, but translating weather shocks into quantitative supply and price signals remains difficult. For most fresh vegetables, the data infrastructure that enables high-frequency crop monitoring in grain markets simply does not exist. There are no futures contracts, no weekly crop progress reports, and no county-level yield estimates. Buyers, distributors, and supply chain managers rely instead on trade reports, informal market intelligence, and experience. However, it remains unclear whether downstream market data can be used to recover supply responses to weather shocks.

This paper studies how weather shocks affect supply and prices in the U.S.\ iceberg lettuce market. Lettuce is the largest fresh vegetable crop in the United States by volume and is produced year-round through a seasonal rotation between California (spring through fall) and Arizona (winter). We construct a weekly dataset that integrates USDA truck shipment volumes, FOB shipping point prices, and gridded weather data from PRISM, covering six production districts over the period 2010--2026.

Our empirical strategy combines market-level aggregation for prediction with district-level panel variation for identification. At the market level, we aggregate all districts into a single weekly time series and estimate price and volume prediction models using OLS, XGBoost, and Random Forest. At the district level, we retain a three-district panel and use fixed effects to identify the causal effect of local weather shocks on prices. Both layers are necessary because aggregation improves data coverage (from 72\% to 98\% of weeks with observed prices) but dilutes the weather signal.

First, simple linear models outperform tree-based machine learning methods in level prediction tasks and perform comparably in change models. The best market-level price model achieves an out-of-sample RMSE of \$4.36, a 14.7\% improvement over a naive autoregressive baseline. XGBoost and Random Forest perform substantially worse than the baseline in level models, but in change models all methods converge to similar performance. This reflects a data-generating process dominated by strong price persistence and approximate linearity, under which additional model complexity does not improve predictive performance and reduces robustness in extreme periods.

Second, weather effects on prices are present but obscured by price persistence in aggregate data. In level models, the three price lags together account for nearly half of total feature importance, leaving weather variables with little apparent explanatory power. When we model week-to-week price changes instead, removing the autoregressive component by construction, weather variables become substantially more important. At the district level, a frost event increases the local weekly price by \$2.33 ($p < 0.001$), while this effect is attenuated in market-level aggregates. The mechanism runs from weather to local supply disruption to local price increases, which then persist through the autoregressive structure of the market.

Third, all results are robust across lettuce varieties. Replicating the analysis with Romaine lettuce produces consistent coefficient signs and comparable, and in fact lower, prediction errors (RMSE of \$2.61 vs.\ \$4.36 for Iceberg).

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:literature} reviews the related literature. Section~\ref{sec:background} describes the institutional structure of the U.S.\ lettuce market. Section~\ref{sec:data} presents the data sources and variable construction. Section~\ref{sec:methods} lays out the empirical strategy. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the main results, including the underlying mechanism linking weather, supply, and prices. Section~\ref{sec:robustness} presents robustness checks, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}
% ============================================================

This paper contributes to four related strands of literature: the econometrics of weather and agricultural production, the transmission of weather shocks to prices, climate impacts on specialty crops, and the use of machine learning in applied economics.

A large literature studies how weather affects agricultural production, primarily through crop yields. \citet{schlenker2009nonlinear} show that the relationship between temperature and yields of major U.S.\ crops is highly nonlinear, with extreme heat driving large output losses. Subsequent work extends this framework using improved weather measures and broader datasets \citep[e.g.,][]{roberts2013agronomic}, and finds that weather variation explains a substantial share of yield fluctuations. More recent studies document that climate change has already reduced global agricultural productivity \citep[e.g.,][]{lobell2011climate, ortiz2021anthropogenic}. However, this approach relies critically on high-frequency yield data, which are typically available only for major row crops at annual frequency and are not available for most fresh vegetables such as lettuce.

A related literature examines how weather shocks translate into market outcomes such as prices. Early evidence is provided by \citet{roll1984orange}, who show that futures prices respond to weather shocks. \citet{roberts2009world} and \citet{roberts2013identifying} estimate supply and demand elasticities using weather-driven yield shocks, finding that demand is highly inelastic and that small supply disruptions generate large price movements. More recent work shows that prices also respond to expectations about weather before harvest \citep{letta2022weather}. Most of this literature relies on yield shocks to identify price effects. In contrast, we show that weather effects on prices can be identified directly from market outcomes, and that these effects are attenuated in aggregate data due to price persistence.

Despite their economic importance and greater sensitivity to short-run weather variation, perishable specialty crops have received relatively little attention in this literature. \citet{lobell2007california} estimate climate effects on yields of several California crops, including lettuce, using annual data, while \citet{kistner2018vulnerability} document that specialty crops are particularly sensitive to extreme weather events such as freezes and excess rainfall. These studies focus on annual yield responses. We extend this work by using weekly data to study short-run dynamics and by focusing on market outcomes, prices and shipment volumes, which provide a high-frequency proxy for supply in settings where yield data are unavailable.

Finally, a growing literature examines when machine learning methods improve upon traditional econometric approaches. \citet{mullainathan2017machine} emphasize that machine learning excels in prediction tasks with complex, nonlinear relationships, while economic structure remains essential for interpretation. \citet{storm2020machine} show that machine learning methods perform well in high-dimensional agricultural prediction settings, and \citet{crane2018machine} find gains in crop yield prediction when nonlinearities are important. Our results provide a counterpoint. In the lettuce market, tree-based methods (XGBoost and Random Forest) underperform both OLS and a naive baseline. This reflects the fact that price dynamics are dominated by a strong autoregressive component that is effectively captured by linear models. Once persistence is removed by differencing, all methods perform similarly, suggesting that nonlinearities do not play a large role in predictive performance in this setting. More broadly, our findings contribute to understanding when additional model flexibility improves predictive performance and when simple linear models are sufficient.

\section{Market Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================

The U.S.\ lettuce market is highly geographically concentrated, with California and Arizona accounting for over 95\% of domestic production \citep{usda_nass_vegetables}. Production is organized around a seasonal geographic rotation between two primary regions.

\textbf{Salinas-Watsonville, California} is the dominant production district. Located in the Salinas Valley on the central California coast, it benefits from a mild maritime climate well suited to lettuce during the spring, summer, and fall months (roughly April through November, corresponding to weeks 15--50 of the year). It supplies the majority of national shipments during this period.

\textbf{Western Arizona (Yuma)} takes over during the winter months (roughly November through March, weeks 45--15). The region's warm desert climate allows lettuce production when Salinas is too cold, and shipment volumes are comparable to Salinas during peak winter weeks.

\textbf{Imperial Valley, California} serves as a supplementary desert production district, with a seasonal pattern similar to Yuma but smaller volumes. Three other California districts, Santa Maria, Oxnard, and the San Joaquin Valley, contribute smaller and more intermittent shipments.

The annual rotation between California and Arizona is largely calendar-driven and follows a stable pattern over time. Production transitions occur around weeks 15--20 (as Salinas ramps up and Arizona winds down) and weeks 45--50 (as Salinas winds down and Arizona ramps up). These transition periods are associated with temporary supply contractions and elevated price volatility. They are also particularly sensitive to weather shocks, as supply is already constrained during the shift between regions. Figure~\ref{fig:seasonal_pattern} illustrates this seasonal rotation. Salinas dominates shipments from roughly weeks 15--50, while Western Arizona dominates the winter weeks. Price spikes are concentrated around the transition periods, when aggregate supply is temporarily reduced.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig1_seasonal_pattern.pdf}
\caption{Seasonal patterns in shipment volume and prices by production district. Panel (a) shows average weekly shipment volume and panel (b) shows average FOB prices by week of year. Gray bands indicate the spring (weeks 14--18) and fall (weeks 44--50) transition periods.}
\label{fig:seasonal_pattern}
\end{figure}

Lettuce has a short growing cycle of approximately 60 to 90 days from planting to harvest. Unlike annual grain crops, production occurs on a continuous, rolling basis throughout the season, with fields replanted multiple times per year. As a result, there is no single harvest event that determines total output. Instead, supply in any given week reflects planting decisions made two to three months earlier, realized growing conditions, and the pace of harvest operations. This structure generates a natural lag between weather shocks and realized supply.

This production environment has an important implication for empirical analysis. High-frequency yield data, which are available for many grain crops at the county level, do not exist for lettuce. There is no weekly or even monthly measure of production by district. Shipment data, which record the volume of lettuce loaded onto trucks at the point of origin, provide the closest available proxy for realized supply. Because lettuce is highly perishable, with a shelf life of roughly one to two weeks, storage is limited and most production is shipped shortly after harvest. Shipment volumes therefore provide a high-frequency measure of supply that is central to our empirical strategy.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% ============================================================

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sources}
\label{subsec:sources}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

We combine three publicly available data sources covering January 2010 through April 2026.

\textbf{Shipment volume.} Weekly truck shipment reports from the USDA Agricultural Marketing Service \citep{usda_ams_truck} record the volume of each commodity shipped by truck from each production district, measured in units of 10,000 pounds. These reports provide the highest-frequency measure of supply available for lettuce. Unlike grain crops, which have annual county-level yield data, lettuce is harvested continuously within each growing season and no comparable high-frequency production data exist. Shipment volume therefore serves as a proxy for realized supply: because lettuce is highly perishable and cannot be stored for more than a few days, most production is shipped within the week it is harvested.

\textbf{Prices.} Daily FOB (free on board) shipping point prices from USDA Market News \citep{usda_market_news} report the price at which lettuce is sold at the point of origin, measured in dollars per carton and before transportation costs. We focus on FOB prices rather than retail prices because they more directly reflect production-side conditions. Prices are reported by district, commodity, and package type; we aggregate daily observations to weekly averages.

\textbf{Weather.} Daily temperature and precipitation data come from the PRISM Climate Group at Oregon State University \citep{daly2008prism}, which provides gridded climate data at 4 km resolution for the contiguous United States. For each production district, we extract daily maximum temperature, minimum temperature (both in degrees Celsius), and precipitation (in millimeters) at the district centroid and aggregate these variables to weekly measures. This construction aligns weather exposure with the timing of shipment and pricing outcomes.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Production Districts}
\label{subsec:districts}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The data cover six lettuce production districts in California and Arizona: Salinas-Watsonville, Western Arizona (Yuma), Imperial Valley, Santa Maria, Oxnard, and the San Joaquin Valley. Together, these districts account for the vast majority of U.S.\ lettuce production.

District activity and reporting vary substantially over time. Salinas-Watsonville, the largest district, ships in 560 of the 849 sample weeks and reports prices in 91\% of those weeks. Western Arizona ships in 429 weeks, primarily during the winter months, with 82\% price coverage. The remaining districts have more limited and irregular reporting: Oxnard reports prices in only 2\% of its active weeks, Santa Maria in 69\%, and the San Joaquin Valley in 76\%.

This uneven coverage has two implications. First, price data are missing non-randomly across districts and time. Second, relying on any single district would substantially reduce the usable sample. These features motivate the dual-layer empirical strategy described in Section~\ref{subsec:dual_layer}, which combines market-level aggregation to maximize coverage with district-level variation for identification.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Variable Construction}
\label{subsec:construction}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Market-level price.} For each week, we construct a shipment-weighted average FOB price across all districts that report a price (Equation~\ref{eq:weighted_price}). Weighting by shipment volume ensures that districts with larger production have greater influence on the market price.

\textbf{Market-level weather.} Weather variables are aggregated to the market level using shipment weights. Market-level weather reflects conditions in the districts that are actively supplying the market in a given week, rather than a fixed geographic average. When production shifts seasonally from Salinas to Yuma, the market-level weather measures shift accordingly.

\textbf{Extreme weather indicators.} Using the daily weather data, we construct three indicator variables for each district-week. The variable $freeze\_risk$ equals one if the minimum temperature falls below 0\textdegree C on any day within the week. The variable $extreme\_heat$ equals one if the maximum temperature exceeds 40\textdegree C on any day within the week. The variable $heavy\_rain$ equals one if total weekly precipitation exceeds 25 mm. At the market level, each indicator equals one if any active district experiences the event.

\textbf{Lagged variables.} For prices, shipment volume, temperature, and precipitation, we include lags at 1, 2, and 4 weeks to capture short-run persistence and the production cycle.

\textbf{Rolling averages.} Four-week rolling means of volume and weather variables capture the recent background state of the market.

\textbf{Market structure.} We include the coverage ratio $C_t$ (Equation~\ref{eq:coverage}) and the number of active districts $n_t$ as controls to capture variation in data quality and the geographic concentration of supply.

\textbf{Seasonality.} Month and week-of-year enter as continuous variables to capture the strong seasonal pattern in production and prices.

\section{Methodology and Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================

This section describes the empirical framework. Section~\ref{subsec:dual_layer} outlines the dual-layer design that motivates the two datasets. Sections~\ref{subsec:market_model}--\ref{subsec:district_panel} present the model specifications, and Section~\ref{subsec:validation} details the out-of-sample validation strategy.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Dual-Layer Design}
\label{subsec:dual_layer}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The U.S.\ lettuce market spans six production districts across California and Arizona, but not all districts report FOB prices every week. In particular, the three smaller districts (Santa Maria, Oxnard, and San Joaquin Valley) exhibit substantial gaps in price reporting. A district-level panel including all six districts would have a price coverage rate of only 72\%, making it unsuitable for time-series prediction.

We address this by constructing two datasets from the same underlying weekly panel. The first aggregates all six districts into a single market-level time series. The second retains the three core districts (Salinas-Watsonville, Western Arizona, and Imperial Valley) as a panel.

\textbf{Dataset A: Market-level aggregate.} Each week, we compute a shipment-weighted average price across all districts that report a price:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:weighted_price}
P_t = \frac{\sum_{i} V_{it} \cdot P_{it}}{\sum_{i} V_{it}},
\end{equation}

where $V_{it}$ is iceberg lettuce shipment volume from district $i$ in week $t$, and the sum is taken over districts with nonmissing prices. Weather variables are aggregated in the same way using shipment weights, so that conditions in higher-volume districts receive greater weight. This yields 849 weekly observations, with 98.2\% of weeks having at least one observed price. The coverage ratio,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:coverage}
C_t = \frac{\sum_{i \in \text{priced}} V_{it}}{\sum_{i} V_{it}},
\end{equation}
measures the share of total shipments for which a price is observed and serves as a data quality indicator.

\textbf{Dataset B: District-level panel.} We retain the three districts with both substantial shipment volume and relatively consistent price reporting: Salinas-Watsonville, Western Arizona, and Imperial Valley. This panel contains 1,466 observations with 78\% price coverage, higher than the 72\% in the full six-district panel.

The two datasets serve distinct roles. Dataset A is used for prediction, where complete coverage and a continuous time series are essential. Dataset B is used for identification, where cross-district variation in weather provides the identifying variation.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Market-Level Prediction Model}
\label{subsec:market_model}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The market-level price model takes the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:market_level}
P_t = \alpha + \boldsymbol{\beta}_1' \mathbf{W}_t + \boldsymbol{\beta}_2' \mathbf{L}_t + \boldsymbol{\beta}_3' \mathbf{S}_t + \boldsymbol{\beta}_4' \mathbf{X}_t + \varepsilon_t,
\end{equation}
where $P_t$ is the shipment-weighted FOB price in week $t$, $\mathbf{W}_t$ is a vector of contemporaneous weather variables, $\mathbf{L}_t$ contains lagged price and volume terms, $\mathbf{S}_t$ includes seasonal indicators, and $\mathbf{X}_t$ contains controls such as the coverage ratio and the number of active districts.

We estimate Equation~\eqref{eq:market_level} by OLS with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors \citep{white1980heteroskedasticity}. We also estimate the same specification using XGBoost \citep{chen2016xgboost} and Random Forest \citep{breiman2001random} as nonlinear benchmarks.

For the tree-based models, we use conservative hyperparameters to limit overfitting. The XGBoost model uses 200 trees, maximum depth 3, learning rate 0.03, subsample ratio 0.7, and L1/L2 regularization ($\alpha = 1.0$, $\lambda = 5.0$). The Random Forest uses 500 trees, maximum depth 6, and minimum leaf size 10. These settings are intentionally restrictive, as the goal is to evaluate out-of-sample performance rather than maximize in-sample fit.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Change Model}
\label{subsec:change_model}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Lettuce prices are highly persistent: the first-order autocorrelation exceeds 0.9, and in level regressions the lagged price explains most of the variation. This persistence can mask the effects of other variables, including weather. To isolate the role of short-run shocks, we estimate models in first differences:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:change_model}
\Delta P_t = P_t - P_{t-1} = \alpha + \boldsymbol{\gamma}' \mathbf{W}_t + \boldsymbol{\delta}' \mathbf{M}_t + u_t,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{W}_t$ contains weather variables and $\mathbf{M}_t$ includes volume, coverage, and seasonal controls. Lagged prices are excluded because differencing removes the autoregressive component.

Comparing the level and change specifications allows us to assess whether weather effects are attenuated by price persistence.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{District-Level Panel and Identification}
\label{subsec:district_panel}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The district panel exploits cross-sectional variation in weather exposure that is averaged out at the market level. A freeze event in Yuma, for example, affects local prices directly but is diluted when aggregated with regions that do not experience the same shock.

Identification relies on the assumption that short-run weather variation affects supply but not demand. Weather shocks influence prices primarily through crop damage and harvest disruptions rather than contemporaneous shifts in consumer demand. This assumption is standard in the agricultural economics literature and is particularly plausible at the weekly frequency considered here, where consumption patterns are relatively stable and unlikely to respond immediately to short-run weather fluctuations.

The district panel specification is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:district_change}
\Delta P_{it} = \boldsymbol{\phi}' \mathbf{W}_{it} + \alpha_i + \mu_q + \eta_{iq} + \nu_{it},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta P_{it}$ is the week-over-week price change for district $i$ in week $t$, $\mathbf{W}_{it}$ is the local weather vector, $\alpha_i$ are district fixed effects, $\mu_q$ are quarter fixed effects, and $\eta_{iq}$ captures district-by-season interactions. Standard errors are clustered at the district level. Identification comes from cross-district variation in weather within the same week.

We also estimate a level specification that includes lagged prices:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:district_level}
P_{it} = \boldsymbol{\phi}' \mathbf{W}_{it} + \boldsymbol{\psi}' \mathbf{L}_{it} + \alpha_i + \mu_q + \eta_{iq} + \nu_{it}.
\end{equation}
Comparing the level and change specifications at the district level provides an additional check on whether persistence attenuates estimated weather effects.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Validation Strategy}
\label{subsec:validation}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Out-of-sample performance is evaluated using an expanding-window approach. For each test year $\tau$ from 2014 to 2026, models are trained on all prior years and evaluated on year $\tau$, mimicking a real-time forecasting setting.

We require a minimum of four years of training data, so the first test year is 2014 (trained on 2010--2013). The expanding window is preferred over a rolling window because the sample is relatively short and additional training data improves stability; we verify this in Section~\ref{sec:robustness}.

Model performance is measured using root mean squared error (RMSE) in price levels. For change models, predictions are converted back to price levels before evaluation. The naive baseline predicts $\hat{P}_t = P_{t-1}$, corresponding to zero predicted price change.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ============================================================

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Market-Level Price Prediction}
\label{subsec:price_prediction}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} reports average out-of-sample prediction errors for the market-level price models over the 2014--2026 test period. The naive baseline, which predicts $\hat{P}_t = P_{t-1}$, has an average RMSE of \$5.11. The OLS level model with the full feature set achieves an RMSE of \$4.36, a 14.7\% improvement over the naive baseline and the best performance among the models considered.

Both tree-based methods perform worse than OLS in level models. XGBoost averages \$6.67 and Random Forest \$6.81, each substantially worse than the naive baseline. This pattern is not driven by hyperparameter choice. The conservative settings we use are designed to limit overfitting, and the models capture meaningful in-sample variation. Rather, lettuce prices are driven primarily by a strong autoregressive component, and tree-based models do not perform well in this setting out of sample.

In contrast, the change models yield similar predictive performance across all methods. OLS, XGBoost, and Random Forest produce RMSE values within a narrow range of \$5.00 to \$5.12, with no model clearly outperforming the others. This convergence reflects the removal of the dominant autoregressive component present in price levels. Once price persistence is differenced out, the remaining variation is weaker and harder to predict.

The year-by-year results reveal the source of the gap most clearly in 2022. That year, an outbreak of Impatiens Necrotic Spot Virus (INSV) in the Salinas Valley disrupted production severely, and the weekly FOB price for iceberg lettuce reached \$94.33, roughly five times the sample mean. OLS tracked the spike with an RMSE of \$7.77 for that year, while XGBoost and Random Forest produced much larger errors, with RMSEs of \$24.37 and \$23.83, respectively. Because the training data contain no comparable price spike, the tree-based models substantially underpredict the 2022 peak. This illustrates a limitation of tree-based methods in small-sample time series settings: they are generally poor at extrapolating beyond the range of the training data, while linear autoregressive models can extend trends through the lagged price coefficient.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Price Persistence and Hidden Weather Effects}
\label{subsec:persistence}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

In the full level regression, the lagged price coefficient is 1.369 ($p < 0.001$), and the in-sample $R^2$ is 0.891. The one-week lagged price alone accounts for 30\% of total feature importance in the XGBoost level model, and the three price lags together account for 45\%, indicating that price persistence dominates the predictive structure. Precipitation is statistically significant ($\hat{\beta} = 0.123$, $p = 0.010$), but its economic magnitude is small relative to the lagged price term.

The change models tell a different story. When we difference out price persistence by modeling $\Delta P_t$ instead of $P_t$, two things happen. First, all methods converge: OLS, XGBoost, Random Forest, and the log-differenced OLS all produce average RMSEs within a narrow range of \$5.00 to \$5.12. The gap between OLS and tree-based methods disappears because the dominant linear feature, lagged price, has been removed. The residual relationship between weather and price changes appears to be relatively simple, and nonlinear methods do not deliver additional predictive gains in this setting.

Second, the relative importance of weather variables increases substantially. Figure~\ref{fig:feature_importance} illustrates this shift using XGBoost feature importance, which provides a consistent measure of the relative contribution of each variable to predictive performance. In the level model, temperature ($tmax$) contributes only 2.8\% of total importance. In the change model, where price lags are excluded by construction, temperature importance rises to 6.0\%, and other weather variables (precipitation, diurnal range) increase as well. This pattern indicates that the weather signal is present in the data but is largely obscured in level specifications by the dominant role of price persistence.

\subsection{Mechanism: Weather, Supply, and Price}
\label{subsec:mechanism}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

This subsection provides evidence on the underlying economic mechanisms using the district-level panel specification described in Section~\ref{subsec:district_panel}. The prediction results above show that weather affects lettuce prices, but that these effects are masked by price persistence in level models. We use this panel to identify how weather affects prices and to explain why market-level aggregates understate these effects.

Table~\ref{tab:district_weather} reports the district-level estimates. Freeze risk is the strongest and most robust weather predictor: a week with at least one day below freezing is associated with a price increase of approximately \$2.3 per carton ($p < 0.001$), and this estimate is stable across specifications (columns M1--M3). Minimum temperature has a significant negative coefficient ($-\$0.15$ to $-\$0.17$ per degree Celsius, $p < 0.01$), consistent with the freeze risk indicator.

Extreme heat is significant in the level specification (\$2.16, $p = 0.008$) but not in the change models, suggesting that heat effects may operate over a longer horizon than week-to-week changes capture. Precipitation is positive but not statistically significant, possibly because its effects operate primarily through volume adjustments rather than directly at the district level.

These effects reflect short-run supply disruptions. Freezes damage crops and delay harvests, reducing available supply and raising local prices. Because identification comes from within-week cross-district variation, these estimates isolate localized supply shocks rather than broader demand shifts.

At the market level, these effects are attenuated for two reasons. First, spatial aggregation dilutes local shocks. A freeze event in Western Arizona has a direct and immediate effect on the local price, but when averaged with Salinas (which may not experience a freeze in the same week), the signal is diluted. This helps explain why freeze risk is strongly identified in the district panel but appears weaker in market-level regressions. Second, price persistence obscures contemporaneous weather effects. Freeze risk is large and significant in all three change specifications but shrinks and loses significance in the level model (column M4), because lagged prices absorb much of the variation that weather explains in the change model.

Taken together, the evidence supports the following mechanism. Weather shocks, particularly freezes, reduce local supply and raise prices in affected districts. These effects are diluted through spatial aggregation and further obscured by the persistence of prices over time. The dual-layer design is not merely a convenience for handling missing data; it is essential for identifying localized weather effects that aggregation would otherwise obscure.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

This paper studies weather-driven supply responses in a setting where high-frequency yield data are not available. In contrast to major row crops such as corn and soybeans, lettuce production is geographically concentrated and occurs as a continuous flow throughout the season rather than as a single annual harvest. As a result, standard approaches that rely on county-level yield data are not feasible. Instead, we use weekly shipment volumes and FOB prices as downstream market outcomes to infer supply responses. Identification relies on the assumption that short-run weather variation affects supply but not demand. Under this assumption, variation in prices and shipments can be used to recover the impact of weather on supply.

We document three main results. First, simple linear models outperform tree-based machine learning methods for price prediction. OLS achieves an out-of-sample RMSE of \$4.36, compared to \$6.67 for XGBoost and \$6.81 for Random Forest. Lettuce prices exhibit strong persistence, and most of the predictable variation is captured by lagged prices. Conditional on this persistence, the remaining relationship between weather and prices appears to be relatively simple, as more flexible methods do not improve predictive performance and perform poorly during extreme events.

Second, weather effects on prices are real but masked by price persistence. In level models, the three price lags together account for nearly half of total feature importance, leaving limited scope for weather variables to contribute. When we model price changes instead of price levels, weather variables become substantially more important. At the district level, freeze risk increases weekly prices by \$2.33 ($p < 0.001$), but this effect is attenuated when aggregated to the market level. The dual-layer approach, combining market-level aggregation for prediction with district-level panels for identification, is therefore necessary to uncover localized weather effects.

Third, these findings are robust. The results replicate for Romaine lettuce, coefficients are stable across sub-periods, and alternative validation strategies yield similar conclusions. Data quality, as measured by price coverage, is also an important determinant of prediction accuracy.

Several limitations remain. The identification assumption that weather affects supply but not demand is plausible at the weekly frequency considered here, but longer-run climate trends may affect both sides of the market. Our estimates are based on short-run weather variation and therefore do not capture long-run climate adaptation. They provide a useful benchmark for understanding climate-related supply risks, but extrapolating from weather responses to climate responses remains an open question. The 2022 virus outbreak highlights that non-weather shocks can dominate market outcomes, and such events are inherently difficult to predict using historical price and weather data. In addition, the analysis focuses on six production districts in California and Arizona; extending the framework to other crops or regions would require additional data and may yield different dynamics.

Despite these limitations, the results show that downstream market outcomes, shipment volumes and FOB prices, can be used to recover weather-driven supply responses even in the absence of high-frequency yield data. More broadly, the paper illustrates how market data can substitute for direct production measures in perishable specialty crops. For participants in fresh produce markets, the combination of publicly available USDA data and simple linear models provides a transparent and practical approach to monitoring weather-related price risk.
